A Transport-Based Geometry of Belief-Cost

What it costs a digital twin to change its mind about a finite world.

Laurent Caraffa

Université Gustave Eiffel, LASTIG, IGN-ENSG · Preprint · 2026 · arXiv:2606.21585

paper (pdf) · arXiv · code

Two postulates, one hyperbolic geometry. A belief is a probability distribution over the world — here a Gaussian bell, a point in the plane: its centre is the best guess, its width the uncertainty. Certainty (, a perfect spike) is the boundary — and it sits at infinite cost-distance (the space is hyperbolic: negatively curved, saddle-like), so it can never quite be reached.

Changing your mind is not a move but a reshaping of that bell, and its cost is a length, in nats. Watch the strip beneath the two panels: to get from to the cheapest way, the bell first opens up — accepts more uncertainty — slides across, then re-tightens (the violet geodesic). The straight route (blue chord) refuses to open up — it slides the bell straight across instead — and costs more. The cheapest change of mind is a detour through doubt. Abstract

A finite machine's digital twin observes the territory only through finite, noisy sensors; we model its coherent output as a belief — a probability density over states, the Bayes posterior, never a point. Certainty — the perfect twin — is denied twice, by observation and by physics, both read off the Fisher information. To make this finiteness geometric — distances, a boundary, a curvature — we model what it costs to change a belief: a belief-cost geometry, optimal transport in Wasserstein space reweighted conformally by Fisher information.

The framework rests on two posed commitments: that revision cost is a scalar price on transport (the arena), and that the price is uniform — one nat (a unit of information — the base-e cousin of the bit) costs the same length everywhere. Uniform pricing selects the Fisher reweighting because transport demotes Fisher information from the ruler of distinguishability to the slope of entropy — the move that sets transport apart from Fisher–Rao. From the two postulates, three results follow on the conformal (essentially location-scale) class, all invariants of one change of cost unit: a boundary at infinite cost-distance (certainty rejected to infinity), the Fisher family (, the characterization of the second postulate), and a rigidity (these geometries are hyperbolic, and the Stam bound crowns the Gaussian). Thermodynamics fixes the cost unit and motivates the framework; the results are geometric, in nats.

The problem

A field C carries a vegetation height h(x). n point sensors at positions return unbiased measurements with known Gaussian noise . The likelihood of a candidate field is ; it constrains h at the n sensor sites. A belief in the sense above is a proper probability distribution over the whole field, so the setup is completed by a spatial prior — the standard choice in geostatistics, a Gaussian process with squared-exponential kernel (amplitude a, correlation range ℓ). With Gaussian noise the posterior is Gaussian and carries a classical name: kriging. Writing K for the kernel matrix at the sensors, , and for the kernel vector between x and the sensors,

The belief is this Gaussian posterior: the map m(x), its width σ(x), and the covariances between locations. Read at one location x₀ it marginalizes to a scalar Gaussian — one point of the (μ, σ) half-plane of the headline figure, where eqs. 3 and 4 apply exactly. Two standard kriging facts carry the lessons below: given the kernel, the variance σ²(x) depends only on the sensor positions and noise levels — the measured values enter the mean alone — and it decreases, weakly, as sensors are added.

Interpretation

Certainty is a boundary at infinite cost-distance. With the default settings, σ(x₀) falls from 0.65 m (the prior) to ≈ 0.09 m at n = 40. The floor diverges as σ → 0: reaching σ = 0 costs an infinite length, in every dimension. Every belief the agent can reach keeps a positive width and stays revisable.

Every nat has one price. In the i.i.d. slice at x₀ (), doubling the evidence divides the variance by two and lowers the entropy by exactly ½ ln 2 nat, at a near-constant step of length. The kriging figure sits below this ideal — correlated sensors are partly redundant — and at the defaults each doubling n = 5 → 10 → 20 → 40 lowers the entropy by ≈ 0.2–0.3 nat, approaching ½ ln 2 as the coverage densifies. In cost coordinates the statistical rate becomes (eq. 3, integrated along the σ axis): a fixed marginal price per unit of distance.

Drip-fed and in-one-go end at the same belief and pay different bills. Bayes reaches the same posterior through every ordering and grouping of the data; the bills differ. With all 40 sensors, the one-sensor updates end to end typically total two to three times the direct revision — the ratio grows with n, and sits around one and a half at the default n = 8. Each leg is billed at its own two-point distance (eq. 4); the sum pays the noise zigzag and the early tightening (moving the mean costs 2/σ per metre: ≈ 3 nats at σ = 0.65, ≈ 22 at σ = 0.09). This is the triangle inequality, read as accounting. The Revise tab isolates the complementary fact: a revision with ΔH = 0 still costs ≈ 6.6 nats, and the cheapest way to pay it opens up first.

In an algorithm

The intermediate positions along a route have a direct operational reading, and it depends on the kind of algorithm.

An algorithm that jumps — conjugate Bayes, one closed-form update — goes from A to B in one assignment. For it, the geodesic is the surveyor's tape of the jump: the distance stays defined the way the Paris–Lyon distance stays defined for a traveler who flies. The intermediate points are the milestones of the reference route.

An algorithm that iterates — variational inference, a Kalman filter, SGD on the parameters of a Gaussian posterior, simulated annealing — holds a state at every step. That sequence is a path in the half-plane; its billed length is at least d(A,B), and the ratio of the two measures the efficiency of the update schedule. The opening of the optimal route has one concrete name per family: the predict step of a Kalman filter (the covariance inflates before the measurement moves the mean); the high-temperature phase of simulated annealing; the learning-rate warm-up of SGD; the loosened Fisher penalty of continual learning (EWC), followed by re-consolidation.

The midpoint of the optimal route is the maximally agnostic state of the trip: at the apex, the old mean and the new mean each sit within about one σ of the belief. The division of labour is clean — the geometry prices every path and identifies the cheapest; the algorithm supplies the schedule that walks one of them, on its own internal clock. The field stays fixed throughout.

Scope

The right panel is the scalar slice at x₀: every linear readout of the field (the height at a point, the mean over a plot) has a scalar Gaussian posterior and lives on the Gaussian leaf, where each displayed quantity is a closed form from the paper.
