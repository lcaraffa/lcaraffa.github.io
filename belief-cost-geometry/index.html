Abstract

A finite machine's digital twin observes the territory only through finite, noisy sensors; we model its coherent output as a belief — a probability density over states, the Bayes posterior, never a point. Certainty — the perfect twin — is denied twice, by observation and by physics, both read off the Fisher information. To make this finiteness geometric — distances, a wall, a curvature — we model what it costs to change a belief: a belief-cost geometry, optimal transport in Wasserstein space reweighted conformally by Fisher information.

The framework rests on two posed commitments: that revision cost is a scalar price on transport (the arena), and that the price is honest — one nat costs the same length everywhere. Honesty selects the Fisher reweighting because transport demotes Fisher information from the ruler of distinguishability to the slope of entropy — the move that sets transport apart from Fisher–Rao. From the two postulates, three results follow on the conformal (essentially location-scale) class, all invariants of one change of cost unit: a wall (certainty rejected to infinite distance), an honest family (, the characterization of the second postulate), and a rigidity (these geometries are hyperbolic, and the Stam bound crowns the Gaussian). Thermodynamics fixes the cost unit and motivates the framework; the results are geometric, in nats.

The two postulates

Finiteness fixes the object — a density, certainty excluded — but leaves the geometry open. Everything below rests on exactly two posed commitments. Each is a choice an informed reader could refuse; each is defended. Every other ingredient is forced, transparent, or borrowed.
